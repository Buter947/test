How to Use the Gibbs Free Energy Calculator (ΔG)

This calculator determines the Gibbs Free Energy change (ΔG) for a reaction, which indicates whether a reaction will occur spontaneously under constant temperature and pressure. It uses the fundamental equation:

ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

The calculator instantly determines ΔG and the spontaneity of the reaction as you input the values.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Enter Enthalpy Change (ΔH): Input the change in enthalpy for the reaction. Select the appropriate unit (kJ/mol, J/mol, kcal/mol).
  2. Enter Temperature (T): Input the temperature at which the reaction occurs. You can use Kelvin (K), Celsius (°C), or Fahrenheit (°F); the calculator will automatically convert it to Kelvin for the calculation.
  3. Enter Entropy Change (ΔS): Input the change in entropy for the reaction. Select the unit (J/mol·K, kJ/mol·K, kcal/mol·K). Ensure the energy component of this unit is compatible with the unit you chose for ΔH (e.g., if ΔH is in kJ, using J for ΔS is common and handled correctly).
  4. View the Results:
    • The calculated Gibbs Free Energy Change (ΔG) appears instantly. You can select the desired output unit (kJ/mol, J/mol, kcal/mol).
    • Below ΔG, the calculator indicates the spontaneity of the reaction based on the sign of ΔG.

Note: You must enter valid numbers for ΔH, T, and ΔS for the calculation to proceed.

Problem: Consider the vaporization of water at 100°C (373.15 K): H₂O(l) → H₂O(g). Given ΔH ≈ +40.7 kJ/mol and ΔS ≈ +109 J/mol·K, calculate ΔG and determine spontaneity.

  1. ΔH: Enter 40.7, Unit = kJ/mol
  2. T: Enter 100, Unit = °C (Calculator converts to 373.15 K)
  3. ΔS: Enter 109, Unit = J/mol·K

  • Spontaneity will indicate: Reaction is at Equilibrium (ΔG ≈ 0), which makes sense for boiling water at its boiling point.

Understanding Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)

ΔG is a crucial thermodynamic quantity that combines enthalpy and entropy to predict reaction spontaneity.

Q: What is the Gibbs Free Energy equation?

A: The equation relates Gibbs Free Energy change (ΔG) to enthalpy change (ΔH), absolute temperature (T), and entropy change (ΔS):

ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

Where:

  • ΔG: Change in Gibbs Free Energy. Determines spontaneity.
  • ΔH: Change in Enthalpy. Represents heat absorbed or released (negative is exothermic, positive is endothermic).
  • T: Absolute Temperature (in Kelvin).
  • ΔS: Change in Entropy. Represents the change in disorder or randomness (positive means more disorder).

Q: How does ΔG predict spontaneity?

A: The sign of ΔG at a given temperature and pressure indicates whether a reaction can proceed spontaneously (without external energy input):

  • ΔG < 0 (Negative): The reaction is spontaneous in the forward direction (product-favored).
  • ΔG > 0 (Positive): The reaction is non-spontaneous in the forward direction. The reverse reaction is spontaneous.
  • ΔG = 0: The reaction is at equilibrium. The rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, and there is no net change.

This calculator determines spontaneity based on these criteria.

Q: Why are the units for ΔH and ΔS so important?

A: The term `TΔS` must have the same energy units as `ΔH` for the subtraction `ΔH - TΔS` to be valid. Enthalpy (ΔH) is often given in kilojoules (kJ/mol), while entropy (ΔS) is often given in joules per Kelvin per mole (J/mol·K). Notice the difference: kJ vs J.

This calculator automatically handles this conversion. When calculating `TΔS`, it typically converts ΔS from J/mol·K to kJ/mol·K (by dividing by 1000) if ΔH is given in kJ/mol, ensuring the units match before calculating ΔG. It then converts the final ΔG result to your chosen output unit.

Q: Why must Temperature (T) be in Kelvin?

A: Thermodynamic calculations, including the `TΔS` term, require absolute temperature measured from absolute zero. Kelvin (K) is the absolute scale. Using Celsius (°C) or Fahrenheit (°F) directly would lead to incorrect results, including nonsensical negative ΔG values at low Celsius temperatures. This calculator ensures T is always converted to Kelvin before being used in the equation.

Q: Does this calculator work for standard conditions (ΔG°)?

A: Yes, if you input the standard enthalpy change (ΔH°) and standard entropy change (ΔS°) and use the standard temperature (usually 298.15 K or 25°C), the calculator will compute the standard Gibbs Free Energy change (ΔG°).
