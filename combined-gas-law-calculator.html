How to Use the Combined Gas Law Calculator

This calculator solves problems involving the Combined Gas Law, which relates the pressure (P), volume (V), and absolute temperature (T) of a fixed amount of gas undergoing changes. The formula is:

(P₁V₁) / T₁ = (P₂V₂) / T₂

This tool allows you to solve for any one of the six variables (P₁, V₁, T₁, P₂, V₂, T₂) if you know the other five. It automatically handles temperature unit conversions.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Choose Your Goal: Click one of the six buttons at the top to select the variable you want to calculate (e.g., "Final Pressure (P₂)"). The corresponding input field will become the read-only result box.
  2. Enter Known Values: Input the five known values into their respective boxes under "Initial State (1)" and "Final State (2)".
  3. Select Units for Each Value: Use the dropdown menus next to each input box to specify the units for every pressure, volume, and temperature value you entered.
    • Crucially: Temperature values entered in °C or °F will be automatically converted to Kelvin (K) for the calculation.
    • Ensure the pressure units for P₁ and P₂ match conceptually (e.g., both atm, or both kPa).
    • Ensure the volume units for V₁ and V₂ match conceptually (e.g., both L, or both mL).
  4. View the Result: The calculated value will appear instantly in the designated result box, displayed in the unit you selected for that variable.

Example Calculation: Finding Final Pressure (P₂)

Problem: A gas occupies 2.0 L at 25°C and 1.0 atm. What pressure will it exert if the volume is changed to 1.5 L and the temperature is raised to 50°C?

  1. Select Target: Click "Final Pressure (P₂)" (this is the default).
  2. Enter Initial State (1):
    • P₁ = 1.0, Unit = atm
    • V₁ = 2.0, Unit = L
    • T₁ = 25, Unit = °C
  3. Enter Final State (2):
    • V₂ = 1.5, Unit = L
    • T₂ = 50, Unit = °C
  4. Select P₂ Unit: Choose atm.
  5. View the Result: The "Pressure (P₂)" field will instantly show the result ≈ 1.45 atm.

Understanding the Combined Gas Law

This law combines Boyle's Law, Charles's Law, and Gay-Lussac's Law into a single expression relating pressure, volume, and temperature for a fixed amount of gas.

Q: What is the Combined Gas Law equation?

A: The formula is:

(P₁ × V₁) / T₁ = (P₂ × V₂) / T₂

Where:

  • P₁ = Initial Pressure
  • V₁ = Initial Volume
  • T₁ = Initial Absolute Temperature (in Kelvin)
  • P₂ = Final Pressure
  • V₂ = Final Volume
  • T₂ = Final Absolute Temperature (in Kelvin)

This law holds true as long as the amount of gas (moles) remains constant.

Q: Why MUST temperature be in Kelvin?

A: The relationship between volume/pressure and temperature in gas laws is directly proportional to the **absolute temperature**. Absolute temperature scales (like Kelvin) start at absolute zero (0 K), the theoretical point where gas particles have minimal motion. Celsius (°C) and Fahrenheit (°F) are relative scales with arbitrary zero points. Using °C or °F directly in the P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ formula will lead to incorrect results because the ratios T₂/T₁ would be wrong (and you could even encounter division by zero if T=0°C).

This calculator automatically converts any °C or °F inputs to K before performing the calculation:

  • K = °C + 273.15
  • K = (°F - 32) × 5/9 + 273.15
If you are solving for a temperature, the result is calculated in Kelvin and then converted back to your chosen unit (°C, °F, or K).

Q: Do Pressure and Volume units need specific conversions?

A: No, not for this specific formula. The formula works as long as the units are **consistent** within pairs. For example:

  • If P₁ is in atmospheres (atm), P₂ must also be in atmospheres (atm) for the result to be correct in atm.
  • If V₁ is in milliliters (mL), V₂ must also be in milliliters (mL) for the calculation to work.

The units effectively cancel out in the ratio. Just ensure you select the correct, matching units in the dropdowns for your initial and final states.

Q: What if one variable remains constant (e.g., constant temperature)?

A: The Combined Gas Law simplifies to the other gas laws if a variable is held constant:

  • Constant Temperature (T₁ = T₂): The formula becomes P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (Boyle's Law). You can still use this calculator; just enter the same value and unit for T₁ and T₂.
  • Constant Pressure (P₁ = P₂): The formula becomes V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (Charles's Law). Enter the same value and unit for P₁ and P₂.
  • Constant Volume (V₁ = V₂): The formula becomes P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (Gay-Lussac's Law). Enter the same value and unit for V₁ and V₂.

This calculator effectively covers all these scenarios.
